Getting back into RSS feeds

14.10.2025

> Like many people, I’ve grown increasingly frustrated with social media. I find myself putting my phone away sooner and sooner while scrolling. Instagram feels over-optimized, bombarding me with the same content whenever I (sometimes accidentally) click a reel. Even with highly personalized Reddit subscriptions, the number of truly interesting posts seems to be shrinking. Honestly, less than 10% of what I scroll through feels worth my time—and that’s without even counting AI-generated fluff.

> Inspired by a great video from pagemelt, I decided to try becoming my own “algorithm.” I wanted a higher percentage of meaningful content and more control over what I consume. That’s when I returned to RSS feeds, which let me bypass over-optimized recommendations and curate my own media.
> The benefits are clear: I reduce distractions, avoid endless algorithmic scrolling, and rediscover content I actually care about. I can prioritize high-quality sources, curate diverse perspectives, and consume content at my own pace—without being pulled into viral trends or repetitive posts.

> Not every site I’m interested in keeps a solid feed—the Google Developer Blog, for example—but overall, the results have been rewarding. I even subscribe to interesting subreddits via RSS, which has reduced my Reddit usage and lets me engage with content directly in my browser instead of through apps.
Currently, I’m using NetNewsWire on my iPhone and MacBook, syncing subscriptions via iCloud. Feeds can also be easily exported or imported across devices. Here’s a small excerpt of what I follow:
